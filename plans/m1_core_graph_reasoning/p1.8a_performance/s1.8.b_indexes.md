## Context

User direction 2026-05-21:

> saturation that produce 150 facts and runs 1.5 seconds IS
> INSANE! Python performance is 1M-10M ops/s. Before
> saturation we have 64 facts and 8 rules. (64×8)² and even
> (64×8)³ both fall short of the time spent. WHAT IS THE
> SATURATION DOING SO LONG?

The answer involves match-bind allocation, NAF re-evaluation,
provenance bookkeeping. Indexes reduce the look-up cost from
linear-in-K to O(1) for the lookup steps that today scan KB.

- **a.** `_facts_by_relation` — fact lookup by relation name.
- **b.** `_negated_facts` — `(rel, args) → bool` for `(not …)`

For each: amortised complexity, who reads it, who writes it.

### Task T1.8.B-idx.2 — Profile

Per [S1.5a.6](../p1.5a_zebra_solution/s1.5a.6_pypy_compat_perf.md)
or independently:

- Time `bench_saturate examples/saturation/...` under CPython
  with cProfile.
- Identify hot spots: which lookups dominate?
- Confirm or refute the "indexes will help" hypothesis.

### Task T1.8.B-idx.3 — Add new indexes (driven by profile)

Likely candidates per the parent README:

- **Index of all names** — universal name → entity lookup.
- **Indexes of names per type** (object / relation / rule / …)
  — narrows the universal lookup.
- **Index of facts by object** — given an object, all facts
  mentioning it (composes with hypgen scoring, S1.5a.7).
- **Index of facts by arity** — `(arity, head) → fact` for
  fixed-arity dispatching.
