## 1. Functionality connected to the name

`(false …)` is the **direct** ⊥: a rule that fires to assert `(false …)`
declares "these premises are jointly unsatisfiable". Where
[`not`](s1.7.12_not.md) needs a `(X, (not X))` *pair* to signal
contradiction, `(false …)` is contradiction in **one fact** — the rule
author asserts impossibility directly, and its provenance DAG *is* the
firing chain that reached ⊥ (the seed for the unsat-core walk). The
engine recognises the head `"false"` as "this fact means the branch is
dead".

## 2. Consequences if removed

The engine loses **author-declared** contradictions — every rule of the
form `:assert (false …)` (collision / arity / domain-violation rules)
would no longer kill its branch. Such rules would have to be rewritten to
assert some `(X)` plus a matching `(not X)` to trip the pair detector —
doable in principle but it forces every "this is impossible" statement
through a two-fact dance and loses the single-fact unsat-core seed. Solve
still works on puzzles whose only contradictions are X/¬X pairs; puzzles
relying on direct ⊥ rules (the elimination family) break. Soundness
intact, expressiveness reduced.

## 3. How it could be implemented in ein-lang rules (or elsewhere)

`false` is the *target* of rules, not derivable *by* a rule from
something simpler — it is the ⊥ of the logic. The reduction "express
`false` via `not`" is real but **inverts** the convenience: `(false)` ≡
the always-false proposition; a rule could `:assert (p)` and a fixed
companion `:assert (not p)` on a reserved `p`, tripping the pair detector
— so `false` is *encodable* as a degenerate `not`-pair. That makes it
reducible to [`not`](s1.7.12_not.md) in principle, at the cost of the
direct unsat-core seed and a reserved proposition. No simpler primitive
exists below the `not`/`false` pair.

## 4. How it could be implemented without sticking to the name

Like `not`, `false` could be a **`Fact` flag** (a `bottom` marker) the
detector branches on instead of the head string `"false"` — relocating,
not removing. Alternatively, fold it into `not` (§3) and keep only one ⊥
primitive: `false` ≡ `(not ⊤)` for a reserved `⊤`. The honest result:
the *⊥ calculus* is irreducible; whether it has **one** primitive (`not`,
with `false` desugared) or **two** (`not` + `false`, today) is a
calculus-design choice, and today's two-primitive form is what keeps
direct-contradiction rules ergonomic and their cores shallow.

## Decision (to record in [S1.7.7 T1.7.7.5](s1.7.7_kernel_purity_analysis.md))

Record `false` as **kernel-irreducible** alongside `not` (the ⊥
calculus). Note the one-primitive alternative (`false` desugared to a
reserved `not`-pair) as a calculus-minimality option, weighed against the
loss of the single-fact unsat-core seed.
